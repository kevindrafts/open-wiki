## Summary

This episode profiles Natalie, a small-business government contractor who wins public contracts and fulfills them through subcontractors. The model is a broker/prime-contractor play: register a business in SAM.gov, find understandable service contracts, get quotes from capable local subcontractors, submit a compliant bid with margin, and manage the contract after award.

## Concrete business ideas

### Prime contractor with subcontracted fulfillment

Operate as the prime contractor on government jobs while subcontracting the field work to qualified businesses. Natalie gives examples such as painting, landscaping, and concrete-related work: the prime finds the opportunity, prices it, coordinates fulfillment, invoices the government, then pays the subcontractor.

Revenue clues from the episode:

- $52,000 charged to the government vs. $41,000 subcontractor cost, producing an $11,000 claimed profit on one short project.
- A five-year maintenance-style contract described as about $800/month profit, or roughly $10,000/year.
- A $962,000 landscaping contract where the subcontractor was described as charging $700,000.

### Help existing businesses win government contracts

A second business model is consulting for capable small businesses that know their trade but do not know how to find, format, and submit government bids. Natalie says many businesses are good operators but not government-contracting specialists.

### AI-assisted bid triage and proposal support

Use Claude or another LLM to read solicitations, answer whether a contract is a fit, summarize requirements, and help draft compliant proposal responses. Natalie says she uses a Claude project for government-contracting work and that AI helped her win a contract.

## Customer segments

- small certified businesses eligible to do business with government buyers
- local contractors that can perform services but do not bid on public work
- housing authorities and agencies issuing service contracts
- subcontractors in trades such as painting, landscaping, concrete, moving/logistics, facility support, or maintenance
- operators with minority-owned, woman-owned, veteran-owned, or other certifications that may qualify for set-asides

## Acquisition / deal-sourcing channels

- SAM.gov contract search and registration
- public award history and past pricing records
- subcontractor outreach around a specific solicitation
- NAICS-code searches
- agency/housing-authority opportunities
- quote requests for smaller simplified acquisitions

## Operational playbook

1. Form/register a business and complete SAM.gov registration.
2. Search for service contracts that are understandable and can be fulfilled by subcontractors.
3. Review solicitation requirements, deadlines, proposal format, and whether subcontracting is allowed or encouraged.
4. Use past award data and subcontractor quotes to estimate price and margin.
5. Contact reliable subcontractors near the job location and confirm they can perform.
6. Submit a compliant bid with all required documents, pricing, and subcontractor details when needed.
7. If awarded, manage communication, performance, invoicing, and issue resolution as the prime contractor.
8. Keep margin but avoid assuming “government pays anything”; price still matters.
9. After gaining experience, niche down into a category where you understand requirements and subcontractors.

## Tools / AI workflows

- SAM.gov for registration and opportunities
- public award-history search for prior prices and incumbent contractors
- NAICS codes for category filtering
- Claude project containing contracting context and bid documents
- AI summarization for long solicitations, compliance checks, and first-draft proposals
- manual verification before submission because formatting and instructions matter

## Caveats / claims to verify

- Government contracting has compliance, insurance, bonding, licensing, set-aside, subcontracting, and performance obligations that can vary by contract.
- The prime contractor remains responsible if the subcontractor fails.
- Some contracts may restrict subcontracting or require a percentage of work to be performed by the prime or similarly situated entities.
- Payment timing can create cash-flow risk even if a contract is profitable on paper.
- The episode's income claims are source claims, not verified financial statements.
- Public claims about small-business set-asides and subcontracting rules should be checked against current SBA/FAR rules before acting.
