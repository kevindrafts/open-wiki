## Summary

This interview profiles Hannah of The Tiny Project, a [[snail-mail-subscriptions|snail-mail subscription]] that grew from a small art/community project into roughly 4,100 customers and about $50K/month recurring revenue. The durable pattern is a tangible, emotionally resonant subscription that benefits from short-form video, unboxing loops, scarce monthly drops, and the sense that the object will never be fully replicated online.

## Concrete business ideas

- **Monthly snail-mail club:** send art postcards, recipe cards, stickers, and small paper goods.
- **Tiny farmers market:** host recurring yard/community markets with vendors, free drinks, music, face painting, and charity ties.
- **Branded client touchpoints:** create tangible monthly mailers for realtors, advisors, bankers, run clubs, or other relationship businesses.
- **Archive sales:** sell past letters or past-month paper goods to late-arriving fans.
- **Male/hobby variants:** classic cars, golf, hunting, survival, DIY, or other niches adapted into tactile monthly mail.

## Customer segments

Stationery fans, junk journalers, gift buyers, community-market attendees, local vendors, realtors, financial advisors, bankers, run clubs, and hobby communities.

## Acquisition channels mentioned or implied

TikTok/Reels, Instagram, Pinterest, waitlists, scarcity, unboxing videos, gifts, community events, local flyers, and word of mouth.

## Operational playbook

1. Start with a tiny batch to test emotional resonance.
2. Use social video to show the origin story and tangible process without spoiling the monthly surprise.
3. Make the paid nature clear on waitlists.
4. Batch stamps, return addresses, folding, stuffing, and packing with simple labor steps.
5. Protect the offline/surprise value while allowing customers to share unboxings.
6. Improve each month rather than overexpanding into unrelated products.

## Caveats / claims to verify

Mailing subscriptions face postage increases, labor bottlenecks, churn, returns, international shipping, IP/art ownership, and spoilage if physical add-ins are used. Reported customer and profit numbers are episode claims.
