## Source summary

John Cheney describes building GenAIPI after discovering vibe coding: a nontechnical founder launched an AI readiness/training/implementation offer for mid-market companies, quickly pivoted from a self-serve AI-IQ test to high-ticket consulting, and later reframed the business as ongoing system installation, strategy, transformation, and education.

## Business ideas

- Fractional Chief AI Officer / AI implementation service for $10M+ businesses.
- AI readiness assessment that leads into company-wide training and implementation.
- AI dashboards/“central nervous system” for CEOs.
- Systems installation around marketing, operations, sales, scraping, reporting, and internal workflows.
- Cohorts/classes teaching employees to vibe-code internal tools.

## Customer segments mentioned

- $10M+ revenue businesses, often $20M-$30M average customers
- CEOs who fear missing the AI shift
- blue-collar and non-tech companies with little AI literacy
- companies large enough to write five-figure checks but not fully staffed with AI/CTO capacity
- operators who can use training to automate internal work themselves

## Pricing / revenue clues

- Initial customer paid $15K after a fast sales call.
- Early packages included $10K, $15K/month, and $25K/month levels.
- Guest reports about $2.5M revenue in year one, over 50% net profit, and a pace toward $7M-$8M recurring revenue.
- Startup cost was described around $400 including domain, email, Stripe, and credits.

## Acquisition channels

- public LinkedIn/build-in-public posting about what he was building
- direct messages/calls to known business owners
- referrals after early wins
- personal social posting that lets business owners connect over non-work interests
- education events/courses that make AI feel approachable

## Operational steps

1. Vibe-code a prototype fast enough to demonstrate the new AI capability.
2. Talk publicly about the build and progress.
3. Message business owners with a FOMO-oriented AI-readiness pitch.
4. Pivot from self-serve funnels to high-ticket calls when buyers show willingness to pay.
5. Sell strategy, transformation, and education rather than becoming a pure dev shop.
6. Install systems and train the client team so they can keep creating internal tools.
7. Use fun first projects to motivate nontechnical employees before deeper training.

## Tools / AI workflows

- Replit/vibe-coding tools for prototypes and training
- OpenClaw/agentic frameworks and Replit in client systems
- Zapier agents, scraping, CRM enrichment, Slack notifications, and guardrails
- CEO dashboards connected to internal APIs/systems
- ChatGPT/Grok-style tools as tutors for nontechnical staff

## Caveats and claims to verify

- High-ticket claims depend heavily on founder sales skill, network, and buyer size.
- Avoid overpromising specific automations before diagnosis.
- Becoming a custom dev shop creates maintenance obligations and delivery risk.
- Client data access, scraping, and internal AI tools need security/compliance guardrails.
