## Source summary

Mike Yoder explains how drone deer recovery evolved into a broader drone business: service work, a pilot directory, YouTube/content distribution, thermal-drone retail, training, and agricultural spray drones. The episode emphasizes category creation, content-led distribution, and high-ticket drone services.

## Business ideas

- Drone deer recovery using thermal cameras for hunters.
- Paid pilot directory/listing marketplace for recovery operators.
- Thermal-drone retail/distribution and training.
- Agricultural spray-drone service for farms.
- Content flywheel around novel blue-collar technology.

## Customer segments mentioned

- hunters who cannot recover deer
- independent drone pilots/operators
- farmers needing crop spraying, especially fields hard for ground rigs or airplanes
- onion growers and specialty-crop operators
- entrepreneurs interested in buying rigs/training

## Pricing / revenue clues

- Early deer-recovery pricing discussed around $550 if found and $450 if not found.
- First deer season reportedly produced $50K in 10 weeks.
- Directory listings are described at about $500/year, with 200-300 pilots in season.
- Agricultural spray-drone jobs include claims of $132K in 24 days and $65K profit in eight days.
- Spray operating cost discussed around $3-$3.74/acre, with charging roughly $10-$20/acre and higher in hard-access cases.

## Acquisition channels

- outdoor/sportsman shows with live thermal-drone footage
- flyers at bow shops and ranges
- word of mouth in hunting communities
- YouTube videos that made the category mainstream
- paid ads and content for drone training/equipment
- in-person farm demos for agricultural spraying

## Operational steps

1. Demonstrate the technology visually; the thermal/zoom reveal is the hook.
2. Validate pricing with people familiar with alternatives.
3. Clarify local hunting, drone, and pesticide rules before operating.
4. Build content that educates the market and creates search demand.
5. Add adjacent monetization layers: directory, equipment sales, training, and services.
6. For ag spraying, show up at farms and demo droplet/application quality.
7. Let farmers specify chemicals; avoid taking responsibility for product recommendations early.

## Tools / AI workflows

- DJI thermal drones and spray drones
- pilot locator/map directory
- content/YouTube as education and acquisition
- agricultural spray trailers and battery/tank rotation workflows
- licensing: Part 107 remote pilot and commercial pesticide applicator license

## Caveats and claims to verify

- Drone rules vary by state, landowner permission, airspace, use case, and payload.
- Hunting regulations can be unclear and changed over time.
- Pesticide application requires proper licensing and compliance.
- High revenue claims depend on seasonality, acreage, equipment, and operator intensity.
