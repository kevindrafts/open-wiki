## Summary

This episode argues for [[tiny-tools-and-micro-apps]] built by unbundling one useful workflow from large platforms such as OpenAI, Apify, Clay, Zapier, Craigslist, Zillow, or BizBuySell. Koerner's thesis is that AI coding tools and API marketplaces let nontechnical founders wrap existing APIs, target a very narrow buyer, and create small paid tools without building full-stack software from scratch.

## Core thesis

The episode frames the opportunity as “great unbundling”: large platforms accumulate many features, while small operators can pull out one job, make it easier for one tribe of users, and charge for convenience. Koerner compares Craigslist being unbundled into marketplaces such as Airbnb/Tinder-style verticals with a new wave of API and AI-platform unbundling.

## Concrete business ideas

### API wrapper tools

Use an existing Apify actor or similar API as the backend, then build a narrow website around it with a simple search/input flow and usage-based or subscription pricing. The example pattern is an Instagram follower scraper: buy the low-cost API call, repackage it as a simple tool, and charge a markup for convenience and positioning.

### Niche lead scrapers

Create specialized scrapers for a buyer segment instead of “all Google Maps.” Examples mentioned include dentist scrapers, Texas small-business scrapers, lawyer scrapers, chiropractors in Atlanta, and local-service lead lists. Buyers include B2B sales teams, cold callers, SDRs, agencies, and local businesses.

Revenue clue from the episode: Koerner suggests simple tools could be sold around $35/month, usage-based pricing, or low monthly subscriptions depending on the data source and buyer.

### Amazon review intelligence for DTC brands

Build a tool where a Shopify/DTC founder pastes a competitor Amazon listing and receives a summarized report on repeated complaints, common phrases, product-improvement ideas, and customer sentiment. This combines scraping plus an LLM summary layer.

### Micro-influencer discovery

Create a marketplace, paid newsletter, or lookup tool for micro-influencers in specific categories such as fitness, food, mom creators, or creators under a follower threshold. Koerner emphasizes that brands often want smaller creators with loyal audiences but lack a clean way to find them.

### Import/supplier intelligence wrapper

Repackage import/supplier data for a more specific buyer, such as Shopify store owners or DTC brands that want to understand where competitors source products.

### Upwork search wrapper

Improve search for agencies, recruiters, or startup founders by filtering Upwork profiles more specifically, e.g. “Ukrainian mobile developers with five-plus years and $50,000+ in earnings.”

### Lead enrichment micro-tools

Build single-purpose enrichment tools, such as converting a first-name-only email record into a fuller contact profile with LinkedIn URL or better metadata. Koerner recommends testing several small ideas and continuing with the one that has the lowest customer-acquisition friction.

### Niche Zillow / niche BizBuySell

Unbundle real-estate or business-for-sale platforms into narrower marketplaces: mobile home parks, self-storage, RV parks, laundromats, car washes, billboards, convenience stores, restaurants, or frozen-yogurt shops. The suggested play is to scrape existing listings, track listing velocity, and build for categories where buyers want a cleaner dedicated view.

### Trend and reputation scrapers

Examples include TikTok hashtag trend scrapers, Trustpilot scrapers, indie job scrapers, Crunchbase/funding scrapers, and weekly trend reports for marketers or brands.

## Customer segments

- nontechnical founders who can use AI coding tools
- B2B sales teams, SDRs, cold callers, and marketing agencies
- local service businesses buying lead lists
- Shopify/DTC brands seeking competitor and supplier intelligence
- recruiters and agencies searching freelance marketplaces
- investors/operators looking for niche real estate or business-for-sale listings
- marketers and brands tracking trends or reputation data

## Acquisition channels mentioned or implied

- LinkedIn posting to a small founder/operator audience
- selling into existing buyer workflows such as SDR lead research or DTC competitor research
- niche SEO around “scraper for X” or “Zillow for Y” terms
- simple cold outreach to agencies, local-service businesses, Shopify brands, or recruiters
- paid newsletters or recurring reports for trend data

## Operational playbook

1. Pick a large platform, API marketplace, scraper catalog, or database with many features.
2. Choose one workflow that a narrow customer already wants.
3. Build a dead-simple website around one input and one output.
4. Use AI coding tools such as Claude, Codex, or Replit-style tools to create the interface.
5. Connect to an existing API or scraper instead of building the hard data layer first.
6. Price for convenience: subscription, per-run, per-lead, or low monthly access.
7. Test several micro-tools and keep the one with the strongest demand signal.
8. Improve positioning and distribution before trying to build a broad app.

## Caveats / claims to verify

- The episode contains broad claims that tiny tools can become $10,000, million-dollar, or even billion-dollar businesses; treat those as motivational claims, not guaranteed outcomes.
- Data scraping and API resale may raise terms-of-service, privacy, anti-spam, copyright, or platform-access issues.
- A wrapper business can be fragile if it depends on one API, scraper marketplace, or platform policy.
- Lead tools and cold outreach databases need compliance review before public promotion.
