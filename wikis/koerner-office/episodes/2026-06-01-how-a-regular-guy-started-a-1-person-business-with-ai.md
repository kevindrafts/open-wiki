## Summary

Phil describes starting a solo AI automation/implementation business without a coding background, beginning with voice agents and warm-network conversations, then turning those conversations into larger AI implementation retainers. The durable pattern is [[one-person-ai-agencies]]: use AI tools to discover client pain points, prototype narrow automations, and sell business outcomes to people who already trust you.

## Concrete business ideas

- AI automation / implementation agency for local or relationship-based SMB clients
- Voice-agent setup for roofing and other local service companies
- AI-assisted content-creation systems built from a business owner's existing content
- Grant-search and grant-drafting workflow for nonprofits
- Product launch management for medical/chiropractic services using CRM, content, forms, and ads
- Bespoke internal apps that can later become niche SaaS products

## Customer segments

- local SMB owners who know they need AI but do not know where to start
- professional-services firms, including financial advisors
- chiropractors / medical or wellness practices launching high-ticket equipment or services
- nonprofits applying for grants
- friends, friends-of-friends, church/community contacts, and prior business relationships

## Pricing / revenue clues

- Investment-firm consulting contract: guest says first six months were for $50,000.
- Chiropractor laser-launch project: $3,500 setup plus $1,500/month for six months.
- Performance kicker: $75 per client above 10 monthly clients for the laser service.
- Nonprofit grant workflow proposal: $1,500 per grant application, $2,000/month retainer, capped at 8% of grant proceeds and billed only after a grant comes through.
- Koerner frames AI agency/client work generally as $500-$5,000/month in a sponsor/community segment.

## Acquisition channels

- Start with contacts already in the phone/address book.
- Text warm contacts to ask for a conversation about their business and AI opportunities.
- Meet business owners in person and listen for pain points instead of leading with a long AI demo.
- Ask existing satisfied contacts for referrals.
- Use an early useful build or free proof-of-concept to create trust, then expand into paid work.

## Operational playbook

1. Pick an easy entry point, such as voice agents or CRM automation.
2. Make a list of warm contacts and request discovery conversations.
3. Record calls or meetings so the operator can listen instead of note-taking.
4. Feed the transcript into Claude or a similar tool to identify pain points, solution ideas, objections, pricing, and proposal language.
5. Build a narrow first solution in tools such as GoHighLevel, Claude/Claude Code, and OpenClaw.
6. Price around the business value and operational burden removed, not around hours spent.
7. Use the first client to uncover follow-on work and reusable niche products.

## AI/tool workflow

- GoHighLevel for voice agents, CRM workflows, forms, and automated client messaging.
- Claude / Claude Code for solution design, contract/proposal drafting, pricing support, and app building.
- Conversation recorders such as Limitless/Plaud or phone voice notes for discovery transcripts.
- OpenClaw agent workflows for grant research and drafting.
- Knowledge bases built from existing recordings, ads, or customer content to create on-brand business content.

## Caveats / claims to verify

- Revenue, contract, and client-performance figures are guest-reported and should be independently verified before reuse as fact.
- Grant-writing, financial-advisor content, medical/wellness claims, and FDA-adjacent marketing all need human review and compliance checks.
- Warm-network selling works better when the offer genuinely helps; aggressive selling into personal relationships can burn trust.
- The guest had not yet settled on a repeatable niche/productized offer, which may limit scalability.
