# He Made $6K in 3 Hours Selling Lemonade

Don from Booze Lemonade describes turning a fresh-squeezed lemonade stand from a family farmers-market side project into a serious local concession/catering business: roughly $250k in annual market revenue, $7 cups with about $1.25 product cost, event packages in the $1k-$3k range, cart rentals, venue partnerships, and adjacent frozen-banana catering. The key pattern is a visually engaging, low-complexity beverage/dessert operation sold through high-foot-traffic markets, festivals, corporate events, weddings, and concession partners.

## Concise source summary

The episode profiles a lemonade concession business that began at Frisco Fresh Market and expanded to Dallas Farmers Market, caterings, pop-ups, cart rentals, concession-company partnerships, and planned retail/storefront exploration. The product is deliberately simple: fresh-squeezed lemon, simple syrup, filtered water, ice, and optional shelf-stable flavors. The business relies on visible production, strong packaging/aesthetic, family labor and hired operators, recurring market booths, and event/corporate bookings. Don also describes a high-ticket frozen-banana/apple-nacho catering job as an adjacent upsell that fit the same concessions workflow.

## Concrete business ideas

- **Fresh-squeezed lemonade stand/concession:** sell 32 oz lemonades at farmers markets, festivals, pop-ups, school events, HOAs, convention centers, and partner venues.
- **Lemonade catering:** staff a lemonade bar for corporate, family, school, HOA, wedding, and other private events.
- **Lemonade cart rental:** rent a branded cart to event hosts or bartending companies, including weddings/cocktail hours, with optional delivery/pickup fee.
- **Venue/concession partnership:** supply product/brand while a concession partner handles labor and pays the venue commission.
- **Frozen bananas and apple nachos:** add a frozen dessert product for catering or market days, especially when the customer asks what else the vendor can provide.
- **Family weekend business:** run booths with family members where legally allowed, using the business as an entrepreneurship project for kids.

## Pricing and revenue clues

- 32 oz lemonade anchor price: **$7** at normal markets, with reported **~$1.25 cost including packaging and ice**.
- Partner venues may charge **$10** because venue/concession commissions can be high, mentioned as sometimes around **40%**.
- Frisco market average day: **~$1,200-$1,500** from 8 a.m. to 4 p.m.
- Dallas Farmers Market: **~$2,500/day**, or **~$5,000 weekend**; booth fee mentioned as **$300 total with electricity**.
- First-year sales: **~$25k-$30k**; later year: **~$250k**, described as mostly across roughly eight active months.
- Best pop-up: eclipse event did **almost $6k in three hours**, with about **$5k profit** claimed.
- Catering example: **$2,900** for a four-hour event with 300 pre-made lemonades at $5/cup plus travel, servers, service fee, and bar rental.
- Typical catering range: **$1k-$3k** for two- to three-hour events.
- Cart rental: **$500/day** promotional direct rental; normal model includes **$250 delivery/pickup** in addition.
- Frozen banana/apple nacho catering: **$19k charged**, with **~$12k profit** claimed after high labor spend.

## Operational steps

1. **Start lean:** table, tent, recipe, cups, ice, lemons, sugar, water, and basic hand tools are enough for a small test.
2. **Dial in product:** use lemon wedges smashed in the serving cup, 1:1 simple syrup, filtered water, ice, and optional flavor puree.
3. **Buy inputs from restaurant suppliers:** Restaurant Depot and Ben E. Keith are mentioned for lemons, sugar, bananas, and larger orders.
4. **Prep centrally:** wash lemons and batch simple syrup in a warehouse/commercial prep setup as volume grows.
5. **Use speed tools when volume rises:** Sunkist wedger, handheld or pneumatic lemon smasher, large syrup batches, reliable ice delivery.
6. **Make production visible:** the lemon-smashing sound/action acts as a draw and reinforces freshness.
7. **Staff multiple locations:** family members and hired operators can run market booths while the owner restocks and handles pop-ups/catering.
8. **Separate service models:** markets for daily sales, catering for higher-ticket weekday revenue, rentals for low-labor income, venues for scale.
9. **Add adjacent products carefully:** frozen bananas require freezer capacity, chocolate warmers, toppings, sticks, storage, and power.

## Caveats and claims to verify

- Health permits, cottage-food rules, youth lemonade-stand laws, HOA rules, and market/vendor requirements vary by city and state.
- Reported revenue/profit figures are interview claims and should be verified with actual P&L, sales reports, market receipts, and tax/fee details.
- Margins depend heavily on lemons, ice delivery, labor, booth fees, travel, commissions, weather, and waste.
- Venue commissions and exclusive vendor contracts can materially change unit economics.
- Market saturation may differ: Don notes little/no lemonade competition in his original markets, which may not hold elsewhere.
- The Texas under-18 lemonade law comment should be checked before applying in other jurisdictions or for adult-run commercial stands.
- Frozen bananas had one very successful catering order but an Oktoberfest test performed less well; product-market fit is event-specific.
