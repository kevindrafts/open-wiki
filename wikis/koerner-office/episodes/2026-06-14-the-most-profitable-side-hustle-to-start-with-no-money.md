# The Most Profitable Side Hustle to Start With No Money

Jackson and Carson describe a raw-land wholesaling model: first ask home builders what lots they already want to buy, then find off-market landowners willing to sell below that builder's buy price, assign the purchase contract through a title company, and keep the spread without personally buying the land. The episode is unusually tactical on builder-first sourcing, seller outreach, direct mail, title/closing flow, lightweight tooling, and why the guests prefer land over houses.

## Concise source summary

The guests present land wholesaling as a middleman business with low startup costs: find builders who need buildable lots, get their target geography/lot-size/pricing "buy box," locate off-market landowners, negotiate an assignable purchase contract below the builder's price, and close through a title company so the seller receives their price, the builder receives the lot, and the wholesaler receives an assignment fee. They claim the model can be done virtually nationwide, often without seeing the lots, and that the hard part is simple but uncomfortable outreach rather than capital or licensing.

## Concrete business ideas

- **Builder-first raw-land wholesaling:** call active spec/custom builders, ask what lots they will buy, then source matching off-market parcels cheaper than their stated buy price.
- **Direct-mail contract offers:** mail landowners a one-page intro plus an already-filled offer/contract; the guests say some sellers mailed back signed contracts without a prior phone call.
- **Neighbor acquisition for land deals:** use owner lookup tools to call neighboring homeowners before/after a deal and ask if they want to sell or object to new construction nearby.
- **Seller-referral program:** after a successful closing, send a thank-you note offering a meaningful referral fee; the guests cite 28-29 referral deals in one year from a $1,000 incentive.
- **Beginner local-market version:** start in a familiar area, find recent new-build listings on Zillow, call the builder/listing contact, document their buy box, then call owners of nearby vacant lots.

## Customer segments mentioned

- **Buyers:** spec home builders, custom home builders, regional/national production builders, and small builders doing 1-30 homes/year.
- **Sellers:** long-time landowners with paid-off or low-basis vacant lots, owners facing rising property taxes, owners who bought land years ago and are not actively using it, and listed land sellers who may prefer a faster net-cash close.
- **Supporting ecosystem:** title companies, real estate agents, surveyors, contractors, and eventual homebuyers indirectly benefit when a dormant lot is turned into buildable inventory.

## Pricing / revenue clues

- Basic example: builder will pay $50,000 for a lot, seller accepts $40,000, wholesaler keeps the $10,000 spread.
- The guests claim their average profit is about **$8,600 per deal** at **90-95% margins**, with typical mid-five-figure lots and occasional much larger deals.
- They cite a best month of **37 deals / $532,000 profit**, **$100,000+ per month for 40 months**, a best year of **239 land deals**, **$2.1M assignments**, and **$1.7M profit**; these are guest claims to verify.
- They frame one deal/month at the cited average profit as roughly a six-figure annualized side business.
- Referral fee example: pay **$1,000 cash** for a successful referred seller lead, justified against an average $8,600 assignment profit.

## Acquisition channels

- **Builder acquisition:** Zillow filters for newly built homes listed for sale; call the builder/listing contact and ask whether they want more lots in that area.
- **Seller sourcing:** property records / list-pulling tools, vacant-lot searches, long-held off-market parcels, TruePeopleSearch-style owner lookup, cold calls, SMS, and direct mail with an offer attached.
- **Relationship-based buying:** keep multiple builders in a market so one lowball buyer cannot dictate prices; bring genuinely good deals to earn daily inbound demand from builders.
- **Referral loop:** post-closing handwritten notes and cash referral offer to previous sellers.

## Operational steps

1. Pick a market with enough builder demand; the guests prefer hot growth markets and areas with multiple builders competing.
2. Find active builders by looking for newly built homes for sale, then ask what they will pay, in which zip codes/neighborhoods, for what lot size and constraints.
3. Record each builder's buy box: price, geography, acreage, acceptable roads/utilities/zoning, and deal-killer constraints.
4. Pull or manually compile off-market vacant-lot owners who have held the property for a long time or bought cheaply.
5. Contact owners by cold call, SMS, or direct mail; make offers low enough to leave a spread below the builder's price.
6. Use an assignable contract and title company. The buyer funds the close; seller receives their agreed price; the wholesaler receives the assignment fee/spread.
7. Diligence the obvious land issues before sending to a builder: wildlife/protected species, wetlands, flood zone, slope, road frontage, main-road exposure, power lines/easements, utilities, surveys, and local rules.
8. Keep follow-up lightweight but persistent: if seller rejects the price, leave your number, document the objection, and move to the next owner.

## AI / tool workflows mentioned

- **Zillow:** free builder discovery by filtering for new-build homes currently for sale.
- **TruePeopleSearch:** free/manual owner phone lookup for beginners.
- **ChatGPT / online templates:** draft an assignable contract, with the caveat that a lawyer should review it.
- **Claude / AI research:** Chris mentions using Claude for land/well/aquifer research as an example of public-data diligence.
- **Guest tools:** Buyer Bridge for land CRM, contracts, title work, buyer data and buy boxes; Land Portal / list-pulling tools; skip tracing; REI Print Mail for direct mail; Reply Smart / Smarter Contact for SMS; ReadyMode / BatchDialer for dialing.

## Caveats and claims to verify

- **Legal compliance:** assignable contracts, wholesaling rules, direct mail/SMS/calling compliance, licensing requirements, disclosures, and title-company practices vary by state and should be reviewed by counsel.
- **Marketing claims:** revenue, profit, monthly consistency, deal counts, and margin claims are self-reported by guests.
- **Market dependency:** the playbook appears strongest where builders are actively competing for lots; rural/slow markets may be harder to exit.
- **Land-specific diligence:** protected species, wetlands, flood zones, road type, utilities, slope, easements, and local building restrictions can kill deals.
- **Tax burden:** high-margin assignment income can create large tax bills with limited deductions.
- **Ethical/pricing risk:** seller offers should be defensible and transparent enough that sellers understand speed/net-cash tradeoffs versus listing traditionally.
