# Food and beverage concessions

Food and beverage concessions are simple, high-throughput local businesses that sell snacks, drinks, or desserts at markets, festivals, private events, schools, venues, and other high-foot-traffic locations. The Koerner Office examples emphasize low-complexity products, visible preparation, event-driven demand, and multiple distribution modes: market booths, catering, rentals, and concession partnerships.

## Patterns

- **Simple ingredient stack:** choose products with few inputs, clear prep, and low unit cost, such as lemonade, frozen bananas, or apple nachos.
- **Visible production as marketing:** fresh squeezing, smashing, dipping, or topping makes the stand itself a sales asset.
- **High-foot-traffic distribution:** farmers markets, city events, festivals, schools, HOAs, graduations, trade shows, convention centers, and venues can concentrate demand.
- **Multiple revenue modes:** daily booth sales, staffed catering, drop-off/rental carts, and venue/concession partnerships each change labor and margin.
- **Adjacent menu expansion:** add products that share customers, suppliers, storage, and event workflows, but validate each event type separately.
- **Weather and seasonality exposure:** outdoor beverage revenue can swing with weather; indoor venues and catering can smooth demand.

## Implementation checklist

1. Pick a narrow product with obvious event demand and simple operations.
2. Test with a table/tent or basic cart before overbuilding.
3. Price against local alternatives and perceived value, not just cost-plus.
4. Source from restaurant suppliers and standardize prep batches.
5. Make the production process visible and fast enough for lines.
6. Capture recurring locations first, then layer on private events and rentals.
7. Track true costs: ingredients, ice, packaging, labor, booth fees, travel, commissions, permits, storage, waste, and equipment.
8. Verify local permits, health rules, youth-stand laws, market applications, and venue exclusivity before operating.
