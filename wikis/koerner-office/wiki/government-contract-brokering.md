# Government Contract Brokering

Government contract brokering is a prime-contractor model where a small business wins public contracts and fulfills the work through qualified subcontractors, keeping the spread between the government price and subcontractor cost. In the Koerner Office example, the edge is learning SAM.gov, reading solicitations, finding reliable subcontractors, pricing with past award data, and submitting compliant bids.

## Pattern

A government-contract brokering workflow usually has:

- a registered business eligible to bid on government work
- SAM.gov registration and opportunity search
- a service contract that can be understood and subcontracted
- local subcontractors willing to quote and perform the work
- prior-award research to estimate pricing
- a compliant proposal and clear disclosure where subcontracting is required
- contract management after award

This is related to [[boring-business-ideas]] because many opportunities are ordinary services, and to [[ai-enabled-service-businesses]] when LLMs help triage solicitations or draft proposal language.

## Operational steps

1. Register the business and complete SAM.gov setup.
2. Search for understandable service contracts and relevant NAICS categories.
3. Read the solicitation for requirements, deadlines, formatting, and subcontracting rules.
4. Get quotes from responsive, capable subcontractors near the job.
5. Compare quotes and prior government award data to set a price with margin.
6. Submit the bid in the required format.
7. If awarded, manage communication, performance, invoicing, and issue resolution.
8. Build repeatability by niching down after a few wins.

## Subcontractor criteria

- responsive before the bid deadline
- relevant experience and public reputation
- able to work at the required location or facility
- honest pricing and willingness to solve problems
- clear understanding that the prime remains responsible to the government

## Caveats

- Government contracting rules are complex and change by contract, agency, category, certification, and set-aside.
- Prime contractors may have limits on how much work they can subcontract.
- Cash-flow risk exists if payment is delayed or if the subcontractor needs payment before the government pays.
- The prime is accountable if the subcontractor underperforms.
- Licensing, bonding, insurance, facility access, and certifications may be required.
