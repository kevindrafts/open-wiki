# Raw land wholesaling

Raw land wholesaling is a middleman real-estate model where an operator identifies buildable vacant lots that professional buyers already want, negotiates an assignable purchase contract with the landowner, and earns the spread or assignment fee when the buyer closes through a title company. In the Koerner Office interview with Jackson and Carson, the strongest version is "builder-first": validate demand and price with builders before contacting sellers, then source lots below that known buy price.

## Core playbook

1. **Validate buyer demand first.** Find active spec/custom builders or production builders by searching newly built homes listed for sale. Ask what they will pay, where, and for what lot profile.
2. **Document the buy box.** Track target zip codes, acreage, frontage, road type, utilities, flood/wetland/wildlife constraints, and max price.
3. **Source sellers.** Pull lists or manually identify vacant-lot owners, especially long-time owners with low basis, paid-off land, or rising property-tax pressure.
4. **Make offers with a spread.** If a builder will pay $50,000, try to contract with a seller materially below that price so there is room for assignment profit and closing friction.
5. **Close through title.** Use an assignable contract and a title company so the buyer funds the transaction, the seller receives the agreed price, and the wholesaler receives the assignment fee.
6. **Avoid land deal-killers.** Screen for protected species, wetlands, flood zones, slope, utilities, easements/power lines, main-road exposure, surveys, and local zoning/buildability rules.

## Why operators like the model

- It can start manually with a phone, internet connection, Zillow, owner lookup, and a spreadsheet.
- Land has fewer comparability variables than houses: no bedrooms, bathrooms, roofs, tenants, termites, or renovation estimates.
- Builders are professional repeat buyers; a good deal can turn into repeat demand.
- The asset can be sourced virtually, though local knowledge and diligence still matter.

## Revenue clues from source

In the cited episode, the guests claim an average profit around $8,600 per deal at 90-95% margins, with much larger outlier deals possible. They also describe a $1,000 seller-referral incentive after a successful close. These figures are self-reported and should be treated as directional until independently verified.

## Risks and caveats

- Real-estate wholesaling law, licensing, assignment disclosures, SMS/cold-call rules, and direct-mail compliance vary by jurisdiction.
- Builder demand is market-dependent; thin rural markets may be harder to exit.
- Diligence failures can kill deals or damage relationships with builders.
- Assignment income may create large tax liabilities with relatively few deductions.
- Seller-facing claims should be transparent about tradeoffs between fast cash closes and potentially higher but slower listed sales.
