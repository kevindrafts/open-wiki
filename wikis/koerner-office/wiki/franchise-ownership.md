# Franchise Ownership

Franchise ownership is a business-acquisition path where an operator pays for a known brand, operating system, lender familiarity, disclosure data, peer group, and sometimes stronger resale multiples instead of inventing every process from scratch. In the Koerner Office corpus, franchising is presented as useful when the brand adds a real moat—financing access, regulatory know-how, proprietary lead flow, national reputation, or vendor/data systems—not merely a logo.

## Patterns

- Match the franchise to the buyer's risk tolerance, operating skill, income goals, capital, and desired lifestyle.
- Favor concepts where the franchise gives access that an independent operator would struggle to build quickly.
- Evaluate whether the service is too easy to replicate independently; easy-start categories can have weak moats.
- Treat the franchise disclosure document as diligence input, not marketing copy.
- Look for channel relationships: realtors, brokers, carriers, property managers, or route density.

## Examples

- Public-adjusting franchise with claimed SBA-friendly financing and high average revenue.
- Pre-sale home improvement framed around realtor referrals and home-value uplift.
- Window-box/flower subscription routes for affluent homes.
- Turf installation and remote-managed cleaning as additional concepts.

## Caveats

Verify FDDs, fees, litigation, territory rights, financing terms, licensing, unit economics, and franchisor incentives. Average-unit revenue is not a guarantee for a new operator.
