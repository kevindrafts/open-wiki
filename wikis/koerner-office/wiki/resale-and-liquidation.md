# Resale and Liquidation

Resale and liquidation businesses buy underpriced goods from households, thrift stores, local marketplaces, eBay sellers, e-waste streams, or liquidation suppliers and resell them through channels where demand is higher. Koerner Office examples emphasize that the edge is not only spotting items, but building repeat sourcing relationships, understanding defect risk, and finding downstream buyers.

## Pattern

A workable resale operation usually combines:

- price discovery through sold listings, not asking prices
- a category with high sell-through and enough margin
- reliable testing / grading / listing workflow
- repeat suppliers or sourcing routes
- downstream buyers for bulk categories
- cash discipline because inventory can tie up money
- return and defect handling built into unit economics

This connects to [[boring-business-ideas]] because many profitable items are overlooked, and to [[sales-and-distribution-playbooks]] because repeat relationships matter on both the buying and selling side.

## Operating principles

1. Search sold listings before buying anything.
2. Start with low-risk personal inventory to learn the platform.
3. Track buy cost, shipping, fees, defect rate, returns, and labor time.
4. Assume “untested” does not mean “working”; verify source quality.
5. Build a contact list of sellers and tell them what you buy repeatedly.
6. Develop bulk buyers for categories you can source consistently.
7. Diversify suppliers before scaling purchase volume.
8. Prefer categories you can test reliably and ship safely.
9. Watch for regulated goods and platform-restricted categories.

## AI opportunities

- monitor marketplace searches for target categories and price thresholds
- draft seller outreach and negotiation messages
- classify listings by model number and likely resale value
- maintain supplier/buyer CRM notes
- estimate unit economics before purchase
- flag risky categories, missing accessories, or likely testing needs
